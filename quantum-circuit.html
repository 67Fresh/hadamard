UNDERSTANDING QUANTUM CIRCUITS
What is a quantum circuit?
A quantum circuit is a sequence of quantum gates applied to qubits — analogous to a classical logic circuit but operating on quantum states. Each gate is a unitary transformation represented by a matrix. The circuit defines how an initial quantum state evolves into a final state that can be measured.
Why does the Hadamard gate appear in almost every circuit?
The Hadamard gate creates superposition — it transforms |0⟩ into (|0⟩+|1⟩)/√2 and |1⟩ into (|0⟩-|1⟩)/√2. This is the fundamental operation that gives quantum computers their power: a qubit in superposition simultaneously explores both possibilities. Without superposition, quantum computers behave like classical ones.
How does the CNOT gate create entanglement?
The CNOT gate flips the target qubit if and only if the control qubit is |1⟩. When the control qubit is in superposition (after a Hadamard), the CNOT creates a state that cannot be described by two independent qubits — they are entangled. The Bell state H→CNOT is the simplest example: the result is (|00⟩+|11⟩)/√2.
What do the measurement probabilities mean?
When you measure a quantum state, you get a classical result — a bit string like |010⟩. The probabilities show how likely each possible measurement outcome is. They are calculated from the squared amplitudes of the state vector (Born rule). A probability of 50% for |00⟩ and 50% for |11⟩ means each measurement randomly gives one or the other — never |01⟩ or |10⟩.
