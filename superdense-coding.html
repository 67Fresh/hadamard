Understanding Superdense Coding
Doesn't this violate the Holevo bound?
No — the Holevo bound limits how much classical information can be retrieved from n qubits without pre-shared entanglement. Superdense coding uses 1 qubit plus 1 ebit (pre-shared entanglement) to transmit 2 bits. The ebit is a resource established in advance. Entanglement-assisted capacity is a different regime from unassisted capacity.
Why can't Alice just encode 2 bits into 1 qubit without entanglement?
The 4 gates Alice applies (I, X, Z, XZ) each transform the shared Bell state into a different orthogonal Bell state. Bob can perform a Bell measurement — which distinguishes between all 4 Bell states — because he holds the other half of the pair. Without entanglement, you can only distinguish 2 orthogonal states in 1 qubit, so you can only transmit 1 classical bit.
What's the relationship to quantum teleportation?
They're inverses of each other. Teleportation: 1 qubit transmitted + 2 classical bits sent + 1 shared ebit → transfers 1 qubit of quantum information. Superdense coding: 1 qubit transmitted + 1 shared ebit → transfers 2 classical bits. Both exploit the same pre-shared Bell pair resource — they just use it in opposite directions.
Has superdense coding been demonstrated experimentally?
Yes — first demonstrated experimentally in 1996 (Mattle, Weinfurter, Kwiat, Zeilinger) using entangled photons. It has since been demonstrated in various physical systems. The fundamental limitation in practice is the difficulty of maintaining entanglement (distributing Bell pairs) over long distances, which is the subject of quantum networking research.
