Understanding Teleportation
Is this faster-than-light communication?
No. Alice must send Bob two classical bits — which travel at most at light speed — telling him which correction to apply. Without those two bits, Bob has no idea what state he should have. The quantum correlation is instantaneous, but it carries no usable information until combined with the classical message.
Does the original qubit get cloned?
No — the no-cloning theorem prevents it. Alice's Bell measurement destroys the original qubit's quantum information. After teleportation, only Bob's qubit holds the state. The state was transferred, not copied.
What are the two classical bits for?
Alice's Bell measurement gives two classical bits (00, 01, 10, or 11). Each result tells Bob which gate to apply to his qubit to complete the correction: 00 = do nothing, 01 = apply X, 10 = apply Z, 11 = apply both XZ. Without knowing which result Alice got, Bob can't recover the state.
Has quantum teleportation been demonstrated experimentally?
Yes — first demonstrated in 1997 (Bouwmeester et al.) for photon polarization states. Since then it has been demonstrated over increasing distances, culminating in satellite-based teleportation over 1,400 km (China's Micius satellite, 2017). Teleportation of more complex states including superpositions and entangled states has also been demonstrated.
