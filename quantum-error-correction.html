Understanding Quantum Error Correction
Why can't you just measure and check for errors like classical computers do?
Measuring a qubit collapses its superposition — you'd destroy the quantum information you're trying to protect. The syndrome measurement is a clever workaround: it measures the parity between qubits (do they agree or disagree?) rather than the qubits themselves. Parity is a classical property that can be read without disturbing the underlying quantum state.
Why does the bit-flip code use 3 qubits?
Three is the minimum to detect AND correct a single error. Two qubits can detect an error but not locate it — you know something went wrong but not which qubit to fix. Three qubits let you compare Q0↔Q1 and Q1↔Q2. The combination of two parity measurements uniquely identifies all four cases: no error, flip Q0, flip Q1, or flip Q2.
Does the bit-flip code protect against all quantum errors?
No — only bit-flip (X) errors. Quantum errors also include phase-flip (Z) errors and combinations. A complete quantum error correcting code must protect against all three Pauli errors (X, Y, Z). The Shor code uses 9 physical qubits to protect against any single-qubit error. Modern fault-tolerant quantum computing targets the surface code, which uses ~1000 physical qubits per logical qubit to achieve very low logical error rates.
Why do quantum computers need so many physical qubits per logical qubit?
Error rates on real quantum hardware are ~0.1–1% per gate operation. For a useful computation to complete before errors accumulate, you need error correction. The overhead depends on the physical error rate vs. the code's threshold. Current hardware is close to but not consistently below the fault-tolerance threshold, which is why today's 1000-qubit machines have very few effective logical qubits.
