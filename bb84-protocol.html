The BB84 Protocol

BB84 is the first quantum cryptography protocol — proposed by Bennett and Brassard in 1984. It lets two parties share a secret key with security guaranteed by physics itself: any eavesdropping leaves a detectable trace.

The Problem It Solves

Classical cryptography faces a key distribution problem: before Alice and Bob can communicate securely, they need to share a secret key — but how do they share that key securely without already having one? Classical solutions involve mathematical hardness assumptions (RSA, Diffie-Hellman) that could theoretically be broken by a powerful enough computer.

BB84 solves the problem differently. It uses the laws of quantum mechanics — specifically, the impossibility of perfectly cloning an unknown quantum state and the fact that measurement disturbs quantum states — to guarantee that any eavesdropping attempt is detectable. Security comes from physics, not from computational assumptions.

The Two Bases

BB84 uses two measurement bases, each capable of encoding a 0 or 1:

The rectilinear basis (Z-basis): Uses |0⟩ and |1⟩ — the computational basis. |0⟩ represents 0, |1⟩ represents 1.

The diagonal basis (X-basis): Uses |+⟩ and |−⟩ — the Hadamard basis. |+⟩ = (|0⟩+|1⟩)/√2 represents 0, |−⟩ = (|0⟩−|1⟩)/√2 represents 1.

The key insight: if you measure a qubit prepared in one basis using the other basis, you get a completely random result. Measuring |+⟩ in the Z-basis gives 0 or 1 with equal probability — you learn nothing about the original bit.

The Protocol Step by Step

1
Alice prepares qubits. For each bit she wants to send, Alice randomly chooses a basis (Z or X) and randomly chooses a bit value (0 or 1). She prepares the qubit in the corresponding state and sends it to Bob over the quantum channel.
2
Bob measures. Bob randomly chooses a basis (Z or X) for each incoming qubit and measures in that basis. He records his basis choice and his measurement outcome.
3
Basis reconciliation. Alice and Bob compare their basis choices over a classical (public) channel. They do not reveal their bit values — only which basis they used for each qubit.
4
Sifting. They keep only the bits where they chose the same basis. When bases match, Bob's measurement gives exactly the bit Alice sent. When they don't match, Bob's result is random and those bits are discarded. On average, about 50% of bits are kept.
5
Error checking. Alice and Bob publicly compare a random sample of their kept bits. If the error rate is above a threshold (~11%), they know an eavesdropper was present and abort. If the error rate is low, they proceed.
6
Privacy amplification. If a small amount of eavesdropping occurred, they apply classical information-theoretic techniques (hashing) to distill a shorter key that the eavesdropper has no information about. The result is the shared secret key.

Why Eavesdropping Is Always Detectable

Suppose Eve intercepts Alice's qubits and tries to read them before forwarding to Bob. The problem: Eve doesn't know which basis Alice used. She has to guess. Half the time she guesses wrong and measures in the wrong basis. When she does this to a qubit in, say, the X-basis, her measurement collapses it into a Z-basis state — a random result — and when she forwards this disturbed qubit to Bob, he'll sometimes get the wrong answer even when his basis matches Alice's.

This introduces errors. When Alice and Bob compare their sample bits in step 5, they'll see an error rate of about 25% (the fraction of bits where Eve measured in the wrong basis and her forwarded qubit gave Bob the wrong result). Above about 11% error rate, Alice and Bob know the channel is being eavesdropped and abort the protocol.

Eve's eavesdropping → ~25% error rate in sifted key → detectable by Alice and Bob
The security of BB84 doesn't depend on Eve being limited in computational power. It depends only on the laws of physics — specifically, the no-cloning theorem and the fact that measuring a quantum state disturbs it. A quantum computer doesn't help Eve.

BB84 was proposed theoretically in 1984 and demonstrated experimentally in 1992 by Bennett, Brassard, Smolin, and Wootters over a 30cm free-space quantum channel. Commercial QKD systems based on BB84 now operate over fiber-optic links spanning hundreds of kilometers and via satellite links.

Common Questions
Is BB84 used in real systems today?
Yes. Commercial QKD systems based on BB84 (and variants like E91) are deployed by companies including ID Quantique (Switzerland), Toshiba, and China's national quantum network. The longest fiber-based QKD link spans approximately 2,000 km in China. Satellite-based QKD has been demonstrated over 1,200 km by the Chinese Micius satellite.
Does BB84 require a quantum computer to use?
No — BB84 only requires the ability to prepare and measure individual qubits, which is much simpler than building a general-purpose quantum computer. QKD hardware uses single-photon sources and detectors, which are commercially available today. The classical processing (basis reconciliation, privacy amplification) runs on ordinary computers.
What are the practical limitations?
Distance is the main limitation. Photon loss in fiber increases with distance, and amplifiers can't be used (they would disturb the quantum state). Current fiber-based systems work up to a few hundred kilometers without quantum repeaters. Satellite-based QKD extends range but requires expensive space infrastructure. Quantum repeaters — devices that extend QKD range using entanglement swapping — are an active research area.
