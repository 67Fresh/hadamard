The Hadamard Gate

The single most important gate in quantum computing. The Hadamard gate creates superposition — the fundamental operation that separates quantum algorithms from classical ones. This site is named after it.

What It Does

The Hadamard gate (written H) takes a qubit in a definite state — either |0⟩ or |1⟩ — and puts it into an equal superposition of both. The qubit is no longer definitely 0 or definitely 1. It is simultaneously both, with equal probability of becoming either when measured.

Applied to |0⟩, the Hadamard gate produces the state |+⟩:

H|0⟩ = (|0⟩ + |1⟩) / √2 = |+⟩

Applied to |1⟩, it produces the state |−⟩ — the same superposition but with a phase difference:

H|1⟩ = (|0⟩ − |1⟩) / √2 = |−⟩

That negative sign in the second equation is critical — it's a phase, and phase is what allows quantum algorithms to create interference. Gates later in the circuit can use this phase difference to amplify correct answers and cancel wrong ones. Without the Hadamard, there's no superposition to interfere.

The Matrix

Every quantum gate is a unitary matrix — a matrix that, when applied to a quantum state vector, produces another valid quantum state. The Hadamard matrix is:

Hadamard Matrix
1/√21/√2
1/√2−1/√2

To apply the gate, you multiply this matrix by the qubit's state vector. The state |0⟩ is represented as the column vector [1, 0]. The state |1⟩ is [0, 1].

Multiplying H by [1, 0] gives [1/√2, 1/√2] — which is exactly (|0⟩ + |1⟩)/√2. The math and the concept match perfectly.

The Hadamard matrix is its own inverse: H × H = Identity. Applying the Hadamard gate twice returns the qubit to its original state. This is easy to verify: apply H to |0⟩ to get |+⟩, then apply H again to get back |0⟩. This self-inverse property is used in many algorithms.

On the Bloch Sphere

The Bloch sphere is a 3D sphere where every point on the surface represents a valid single-qubit state. |0⟩ is the north pole. |1⟩ is the south pole. Superposition states live on the equator and everywhere in between.

On the Bloch sphere, the Hadamard gate is a rotation of 180° around the axis halfway between the X and Z axes. Geometrically, it swaps the X and Z axes. That's why it transforms the computational basis states (|0⟩, |1⟩) into the Hadamard basis states (|+⟩, |−⟩) and vice versa.

Practically: the Hadamard takes anything at the poles and puts it on the equator. That's superposition. And it takes anything on the equator and collapses it to a pole — the opposite of superposition.

Why It Appears in Almost Every Circuit

Without the Hadamard gate, a quantum circuit is just a classical reversible circuit — it can only perform operations on definite 0s and 1s, which offers no quantum advantage. The Hadamard gate is the on-ramp to the quantum regime. Nearly every quantum algorithm begins with a layer of Hadamard gates to put all qubits into superposition simultaneously, enabling the algorithm to process all 2ⁿ combinations at once.

Some canonical uses:

Bell states: H applied to one qubit, followed by a CNOT gate, creates an entangled Bell state. This is the building block of quantum teleportation, superdense coding, and quantum key distribution.

Grover's algorithm: The diffusion operator in Grover's search is built entirely from Hadamard gates, X gates, and a controlled phase flip. The H gates are what create the interference pattern that amplifies the target state.

Quantum Fourier Transform: The QFT — the engine of Shor's algorithm — is a sequence of Hadamard gates and controlled phase rotations. The QFT converts between the computational basis and the frequency basis, enabling period finding.

The identity H·X·H = Z (and H·Z·H = X) shows that the Hadamard gate performs a basis change. What is a phase flip (Z) in the computational basis becomes a bit flip (X) in the Hadamard basis. This is the foundation of quantum error correction — some error types become easier to detect after a basis change.

Named After Jacques Hadamard

The gate is named after the French mathematician Jacques Hadamard (1865–1963), who studied matrices with this structure — equal-magnitude entries of ±1 — in the context of signal processing and combinatorics. Hadamard matrices have the maximum possible determinant for matrices with entries in {+1, −1}, a property that makes them useful for error-correcting codes and signal processing. Their quantum computing application would have surprised him.

Common Questions
Why is 1/√2 in the Hadamard matrix?
The 1/√2 factor ensures the output state has unit probability — that is, |1/√2|² + |1/√2|² = 1/2 + 1/2 = 1. This is the normalization condition all quantum states must satisfy. Without it, the matrix would not be unitary and the result would not be a valid quantum state.
What's the difference between |+⟩ and |−⟩?
Both are 50/50 superpositions of |0⟩ and |1⟩ — if you measured either one, you'd get 0 or 1 with equal probability. The difference is a relative phase: |+⟩ = (|0⟩ + |1⟩)/√2 and |−⟩ = (|0⟩ − |1⟩)/√2. That phase is invisible to a single measurement but becomes important when the states interfere with each other later in the circuit.
Is the Hadamard gate physically implemented as a rotation?
Yes. In physical implementations, quantum gates are applied by precisely timed electromagnetic pulses — microwave pulses for superconducting qubits, laser pulses for trapped ions. The pulse parameters (frequency, duration, amplitude) are chosen to implement the exact unitary transformation described by the gate matrix.
