WHAT'S ACTUALLY HAPPENING
The Bell state Φ⁺ is written as (1/√2)(|00⟩ + |11⟩). This means the two-qubit system exists in a superposition of both being |0⟩ and both being |1⟩ simultaneously — neither qubit has a definite state until measured. When Alice measures, the wavefunction collapses: if she gets |0⟩, Bob's qubit instantly becomes |0⟩ too. If she gets |1⟩, so does Bob. This correlation is stronger than anything classical physics allows.
THE PHYSICS
Does this allow faster-than-light communication?
No — and this is crucial. Alice's measurement result is random. She can't control whether she gets |0⟩ or |1⟩. Bob also gets a random result. They can only discover the correlation after comparing notes through a classical channel. The correlation is real, but it can't carry information faster than light.
What makes the four Bell states different?
The four Bell states differ in their correlation pattern. Φ⁺ = (|00⟩+|11⟩)/√2 and Φ⁻ = (|00⟩−|11⟩)/√2 give same-same correlations (both 0 or both 1). Ψ⁺ = (|01⟩+|10⟩)/√2 and Ψ⁻ = (|01⟩−|10⟩)/√2 give opposite correlations (one gets 0, other gets 1). The minus signs create relative phases between the superposition terms.
What is Bell's theorem?
John Bell proved in 1964 that no "hidden variable" theory — no classical explanation where the qubits secretly carried predetermined answers — could reproduce the statistical correlations of quantum entanglement. Experiments (most recently the 2022 Nobel Prize work by Aspect, Clauser, and Zeilinger) have conclusively confirmed these quantum correlations violate Bell's inequalities, ruling out classical hidden variables.
