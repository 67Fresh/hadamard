The Four Bell States

Bell states are the four maximally entangled two-qubit states. They form the foundation of quantum teleportation, superdense coding, and quantum key distribution — and they're the first thing the circuit simulator demonstrates.

What Makes a Bell State

A Bell state is a two-qubit quantum state that is maximally entangled — meaning the two qubits have the strongest possible quantum correlation. You cannot describe either qubit independently; they only exist as a joint system.

There are exactly four Bell states, forming a complete orthonormal basis for the two-qubit Hilbert space. This means any two-qubit state can be written as a combination of Bell states, and any Bell state can be distinguished from the others by a Bell measurement.

All four states have one thing in common: when measured, each qubit gives a random 0 or 1, but the outcomes are always perfectly correlated — either always matching or always opposite.

The Four States

|Φ⁺⟩ — Phi Plus
(|00⟩ + |11⟩) / √2
Outcomes always match. Measuring |0⟩ on qubit A means qubit B is also |0⟩. Measuring |1⟩ on A means B is |1⟩. 50/50 between both-zero and both-one.
Preparation: H on Q0, then CNOT(Q0→Q1)
|Φ⁻⟩ — Phi Minus
(|00⟩ − |11⟩) / √2
Same correlations as Φ⁺ — outcomes always match. Differs by a relative phase of −1 on the |11⟩ component. Distinguishable only by a Bell measurement, not individual measurement.
Preparation: H on Q0, CNOT, then Z on Q0
|Ψ⁺⟩ — Psi Plus
(|01⟩ + |10⟩) / √2
Outcomes always opposite. Measuring |0⟩ on qubit A means qubit B is |1⟩, and vice versa. 50/50 between zero-one and one-zero. Anti-correlated.
Preparation: H on Q0, CNOT, then X on Q1
|Ψ⁻⟩ — Psi Minus
(|01⟩ − |10⟩) / √2
Anti-correlated outcomes like Ψ⁺, with an additional relative phase of −1 on |10⟩. The singlet state — the unique Bell state that is antisymmetric under exchange of the two qubits.
Preparation: H on Q0, CNOT, X on Q1, Z on Q0

The |01⟩ and |10⟩ outcomes have zero probability — they never occur. This is what distinguishes a Bell state from a simple superposition of two independent qubits. If Q0 and Q1 were in separate superpositions without entanglement, all four outcomes would appear with 25% each.

The Bell Basis Measurement

A Bell measurement distinguishes between all four Bell states. It's the reverse of Bell state preparation: apply CNOT (target qubit → control qubit), then apply H to the control qubit, then measure both. The outcome (00, 01, 10, or 11) identifies which Bell state you had.

This is the core operation in quantum teleportation and superdense coding. Alice performs a Bell measurement on her two qubits. The outcome (two classical bits) tells Bob which correction operation to apply.

Why Bell States Matter

Quantum teleportation uses one Bell pair as a shared resource. Alice Bell-measures her qubit (the one to teleport) together with her half of the pair. The result determines which gate Bob applies to his half. After Bob's correction, his qubit is in exactly the state Alice's original qubit was in — regardless of distance.

Superdense coding is the inverse: one Bell pair lets Alice send two classical bits to Bob by sending a single qubit. She applies one of four gates (I, X, Z, or XZ) to encode her two bits into the Bell state, sends her qubit to Bob, and Bob performs a Bell measurement to decode.

BB84 quantum key distribution uses the Ψ⁻ Bell state (the singlet state) in one of its variants. The anti-correlation property, combined with the impossibility of cloning quantum states, makes eavesdropping physically detectable.

Bell states were proposed by John Bell in 1964 as part of his analysis of quantum non-locality. The four states are named after him. His theorem showed that no hidden-variable theory can reproduce all quantum predictions — a result confirmed experimentally, most definitively by Aspect (1982), and recognized by the 2022 Nobel Prize in Physics.

Common Questions
If Φ⁺ and Φ⁻ have the same measurement probabilities, how do you tell them apart?
You can't distinguish them by measuring each qubit individually — both give the same 50/50 correlated outcomes. You can only distinguish them with a Bell measurement (CNOT + H + measure), which exploits the phase difference. This is why phase is often called "hidden" in quantum mechanics — it's not visible in probability alone.
Are Bell states useful for real quantum computers right now?
Yes. Bell state preparation is a standard benchmark for two-qubit gate quality on real quantum hardware. IBM, IonQ, and Google all report Bell state fidelity as a metric. Bell states are also used in quantum error correction circuits and are the core primitive in quantum networking protocols being developed for a future quantum internet.
Can you have Bell states with more than two qubits?
The term "Bell state" specifically refers to two-qubit maximally entangled states. For three or more qubits, the analogous states are called GHZ states (Greenberger-Horne-Zeilinger states) and W states. The circuit simulator includes a GHZ state preset — you'll see three-way correlations where measuring one qubit determines the outcomes of the other two.
